1.1 The International System of Units

The laws of physics are expressed in terms of basic quantities that require a clear definition for the purpose of measurements. Among these measured quantities are length, time, mass, temperature, etc.

In order to describe any physical quantity, we first have to define a unit of measurement (which was among the earliest tools invented by humans), i.e. a measure that is defined to be exactly 1.0. After that, we define a standard for this quantity, i.e. a reference to compare all other examples of the same physical quantity.

SI Base Units

Seven physical quantities have been selected as base quantities in the 14th General Conference on Weights and Measurements, held in France in 1971. These quantities form the basis of the International System of Units, abbreviated SI (from its French name Système International) and popularly known as the metric system.

Quantity Unit Name Unit Symbol
Length Meter m
Time Second s
Mass Kilogram kg
Temperature Kelvin K
Electric current Ampere A
Amount of substance Mole mol
Luminous intensity Candela cd

Many SI derived units are defined in terms of the first three quantities of Table 1.1. For example, the SI unit for force, called the newton (abbreviated N), is defined in terms of the base units of mass, length, and time. Thus, as we will see from the study of Newton's second law, the unit of force is given by:

1 N = 1 kg·m/s²

1.2 Unit Conversions and Significant Figures

We often need to change units in which a physical quantity is expressed. We do that by using a method called chain-link conversion, in which we multiply by a conversion factor that equals unity. For example, because 1 minute and 60 seconds are identical time intervals, then we can write:

1 min / 60 s = 1 and 60 s / 1 min = 1

Example 1.1: Unit Conversion

Convert the following: (a) 1 kilometer per hour to meter per second, (b) 1 mile per hour to meter per second, and (c) 1 mile per hour to kilometer per hour [to a good approximation 1 mi = 1.609 km].

Solution:

1 (a) To convert the speed from the kilometers per hour unit to meters per second unit, we write:

1 km/h = (1 km/h) × (1000 m / 1 km) × (1 h / 3600 s) = 0.278 m/s

2 (b) To convert from miles per hour to meters per second, we write:

1 mi/h = (1 mi/h) × (1609 m / 1 mi) × (1 h / 3600 s) = 0.447 m/s

3 (c) To convert from miles per hour to kilometers per hour, we write:

1 mi/h = (1 mi/h) × (1.609 km / 1 mi) = 1.609 km/h

Significant Figures

Significant figures represent the precision of a measurement. The rules for determining significant figures are:

  • All non-zero digits are significant
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant
  • Leading zeros are not significant
  • Trailing zeros in a number with a decimal point are significant

Example 1.2: Significant Figures

Determine the number of significant figures in the following measurements:

1 0.00345 m → 3 significant figures (leading zeros are not significant)

2 120.05 kg → 5 significant figures (zeros between non-zero digits are significant)

3 0.500 s → 3 significant figures (trailing zeros after decimal are significant)

4 1500 m → 2 significant figures (trailing zeros without decimal are ambiguous)

How many significant figures are in the measurement 0.004050 m?

1.3 Dimensional Analysis

The concept of dimensionality is important in understanding physics and in solving physics problems. For example, the addition or subtraction of quantities with different dimensions makes no sense, i.e. 2 kg plus 8 s is meaningless.

Actually, physical equations must be dimensionally consistent. For example, the equation giving the position of a freely falling body is given by:

x = v₀t + ½gt²

where x is the position (length), v₀ the initial speed (length/time), g is the acceleration due to gravity (length/time²), and t is time. If we analyze the equation dimensionally, we have:

L = (L/T) × T + (L/T²) × T² = L + L

Example 1.3: Dimensional Analysis

Use dimensional analysis to show that the expression v = v₀ + at is dimensionally correct, where v and v₀ represent velocities, a is acceleration, and t is a time interval.

Solution:

1 Since L/T is the dimension of v and v₀, and the dimension of a is L/T², then when we analyze the equation v = v₀ + at dimensionally, we have:

L/T = L/T + (L/T²) × T = L/T + L/T

Thus, the expression v = v₀ + at is dimensionally correct.

Example 1.4: Dimensional Analysis with Unknowns

A particle moves with a constant speed v in a circular orbit of radius r. Given that the magnitude of the acceleration a is proportional to some power of r, say rᵐ, and some power of v, say vⁿ, then determine the powers of r and v.

Solution:

1 Assume that the variables of the problem can be expressed mathematically by the following relation:

a = k rᵐvⁿ

2 With the known dimensions of r, v, and a we analyze the dimensions of the above relation as follows:

L/T² = Lᵐ × (L/T)ⁿ = Lᵐ⁺ⁿ/Tⁿ

3 This dimensional equation would be balanced only when:

m + n = 1 and n = 2

4 Thus:

m = -1

Therefore, we can rewrite the acceleration as follows: a = k r⁻¹v² = k v²/r

1.4 Vectors and Vector Operations

Vectors are quantities that have both magnitude and direction, such as displacement, velocity, and force. Scalars are quantities that have only magnitude, such as mass, temperature, and time.

Vector Operations

Vectors can be added, subtracted, and multiplied in specific ways:

  • Vector Addition: The sum of two vectors A and B is a vector C = A + B
  • Vector Subtraction: The difference of two vectors A and B is a vector C = A - B
  • Scalar Multiplication: Multiplying a vector by a scalar changes its magnitude but not its direction
  • Dot Product: A · B = |A||B|cosθ (results in a scalar)
  • Cross Product: A × B = |A||B|sinθ (results in a vector perpendicular to both A and B)

Example 1.5: Vector Operations

Given two vectors: A = 3i + 4j and B = -2i + 6j, calculate:

Solution:

1 Magnitude of vector A:

|A| = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5

2 Dot product A · B:

A · B = (3)(-2) + (4)(6) = -6 + 24 = 18

3 Cross product A × B (in 2D, gives a scalar magnitude):

A × B = (3)(6) - (4)(-2) = 18 + 8 = 26

4 Angle between vectors A and B:

cosθ = (A · B) / (|A||B|) = 18 / (5 × √((-2)² + 6²)) = 18 / (5 × √40) ≈ 18 / 31.62 ≈ 0.569
θ = cos⁻¹(0.569) ≈ 55.3°

1.5 Practice Problems

Problem Set 1: Units and Conversions

  1. A carpet is to be installed in a room whose length is measured to be 13.71 m and whose width is measured to be 4.46 m. Find the area of the room.

    Hint: Area = length × width. Pay attention to significant figures.

  2. A worker is to paint the walls of a square room 8.00 ft high and 12 ft along each side. What surface area in square meters must she cover?

    Hint: First calculate the area in square feet, then convert to square meters. Remember 1 ft = 0.3048 m.

  3. One gallon of paint (volume = 3.78 × 10⁻³ m³) covers an area of 25.0 m². What is the thickness of the paint on the wall?

    Hint: Thickness = Volume / Area. Make sure all units are consistent.

  4. A rectangular plate has a length of (21.3 ± 0.2) cm and a width of (9.8 ± 0.1) cm. Calculate the area of the plate including its uncertainty.

    Hint: For multiplication, the relative uncertainty is the sum of the relative uncertainties of the factors.

Problem Set 2: Dimensional Analysis

  1. Analyze the following equations to determine whether dimensionally correct or not:

    • v = u + ax
    • y = (2m)cos(kx) where k = 2m⁻¹

    Hint: Check if all terms in the equation have the same dimensions.

  2. A given formula is derived for the velocity of an object v that varies with time expressed as v = at² + bt + c where velocity v and time t are expressed in terms of SI units; determine the units of constants a, b and c in the given equation.

    Hint: Each term must have the same dimensions (L/T for velocity).

  3. The centripetal force F acting on a particle depends on the mass m of the particle, its velocity v and radius r of the circle. Derive dimensionally the formula for force F.

    Hint: Assume F = k mᵃ vᵇ rᶜ and solve for a, b, c using dimensional analysis.

Problem Set 3: Vectors

  1. Given vectors A = 2i + 3j - k and B = -i + 2j + 3k, calculate:

    • A + B
    • A - B
    • A · B
    • A × B
    • The angle between A and B

    Hint: Remember the formulas for dot and cross products in 3D.

  2. A particle moves from point A (2, 3) to point B (5, 7) in a coordinate system. Find the displacement vector and its magnitude.

    Hint: Displacement vector = final position - initial position.
