Chapter 1: Introduction to Economics

Basic concepts and fundamental principles

What is Economics?

Economics is the study of how individuals, businesses, and societies make choices about allocating scarce resources to satisfy their unlimited wants.

At its core, economics addresses three fundamental questions:

  • What to produce? Which goods and services should be created?
  • How to produce? What methods and resources should be used?
  • For whom to produce? Who will receive the goods and services?

Key Economic Concepts

Scarcity

Scarcity is the fundamental economic problem of having seemingly unlimited human wants in a world of limited resources. It means that society has insufficient productive resources to fulfill all human wants and needs.

Example:

A student has $50 to spend. They want to buy both a textbook ($40) and attend a concert ($30). Due to scarcity of money, they must choose between these options.

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative that is given up when making a choice. It's what you sacrifice when you choose one option over another.

Example:

If you spend 3 hours studying economics instead of working a part-time job that pays $15/hour, your opportunity cost is $45 (the money you could have earned).

Factors of Production

These are the resources used to produce goods and services:

Land
Natural resources (water, minerals, forests)
Labor
Human effort and skills
Capital
Tools, machinery, buildings
Entrepreneurship
Innovation and risk-taking

Microeconomics vs Macroeconomics

Microeconomics

Studies individual economic units like households, firms, and industries.

  • Supply and demand
  • Consumer behavior
  • Production costs
  • Market structures

Macroeconomics

Studies the economy as a whole and its aggregates.

  • National income
  • Inflation
  • Unemployment
  • Economic growth

Chapter 2: Supply and Demand

Market forces and price determination

The Law of Demand

The law of demand states that, all other factors being equal, as the price of a good or service increases, the quantity demanded decreases, and vice versa.

Example:

If the price of pizza increases from $10 to $15, people will buy less pizza and might choose alternatives like burgers or sandwiches instead.

The Law of Supply

The law of supply states that, all other factors being equal, as the price of a good or service increases, the quantity supplied increases, and vice versa.

Example:

If the price of coffee rises, coffee producers will be motivated to grow and sell more coffee to maximize their profits.

Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs when the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied at a particular price. This is the point where the demand and supply curves intersect.

Surplus
Occurs when quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded (price is above equilibrium)
Shortage
Occurs when quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied (price is below equilibrium)

Chapter 1: Algebra Fundamentals

Equations, inequalities, and algebraic manipulation

Linear Equations

A linear equation is an equation that can be written in the form:

\( ax + b = 0 \)

where \( a \) and \( b \) are constants and \( a \neq 0 \). The solution to a linear equation is the value of \( x \) that makes the equation true.

Solving Linear Equations

To solve a linear equation, we isolate the variable on one side of the equation:

Example:

Solve \( 3x + 5 = 14 \)

\( 3x + 5 = 14 \)
\( 3x = 14 - 5 \)
\( 3x = 9 \)
\( x = \frac{9}{3} \)
\( x = 3 \)

Business Application

Linear equations are commonly used in business to calculate break-even points:

Example:

A company sells a product for $20 each. The fixed costs are $500 and the variable cost per unit is $5. How many units must be sold to break even?

\( 20x = 500 + 5x \)
\( 20x - 5x = 500 \)
\( 15x = 500 \)
\( x = \frac{500}{15} \approx 33.33 \)

The company needs to sell 34 units to break even.

Inequalities

An inequality is a mathematical statement that compares two expressions using inequality symbols:

\( < \)
Less than
\( > \)
Greater than
\( \leq \)
Less than or equal to
\( \geq \)
Greater than or equal to

Solving Inequalities

Solving inequalities is similar to solving equations, with one important exception:

Important Rule:

When you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you must reverse the inequality sign.

Example:

Solve \( -2x + 3 < 7 \)

\( -2x + 3 < 7 \)
\( -2x < 7 - 3 \)
\( -2x < 4 \)
\( x > \frac{4}{-2} \) (sign reversed)
\( x > -2 \)

Systems of Equations

A system of equations is a set of two or more equations with the same variables. The solution to a system is the set of values that satisfy all equations simultaneously.

Solving by Substitution

Example:

Solve the system:

\( y = 2x + 1 \)
\( 3x + 2y = 16 \)

Substitute the first equation into the second:

\( 3x + 2(2x + 1) = 16 \)
\( 3x + 4x + 2 = 16 \)
\( 7x + 2 = 16 \)
\( 7x = 14 \)
\( x = 2 \)

Now substitute back to find y:

\( y = 2(2) + 1 = 5 \)

Solution: \( x = 2, y = 5 \)

Chapter 2: Functions and Graphs

Linear, quadratic, and their applications

What is a Function?

A function is a relation between a set of inputs (domain) and a set of possible outputs (range) with the property that each input is related to exactly one output.

\( f(x) = y \)

where \( x \) is the input, \( f \) is the function, and \( y \) is the output.

Function Notation

Functions are typically written using notation like \( f(x) \), which is read as "f of x".

Example:

If \( f(x) = 2x + 3 \), then:

\( f(1) = 2(1) + 3 = 5 \)
\( f(4) = 2(4) + 3 = 11 \)
\( f(-2) = 2(-2) + 3 = -1 \)

Vertical Line Test

A graph represents a function if and only if no vertical line intersects the graph at more than one point.

Linear Functions

A linear function has the form:

\( f(x) = mx + b \)

where \( m \) is the slope and \( b \) is the y-intercept.

Slope

The slope measures the steepness of a line and is calculated as:

\( m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)
\( m > 0 \)
Increasing
\( m < 0 \)
Decreasing
\( m = 0 \)
Horizontal
\( m \) undefined
Vertical

Business Application

Linear functions are used to model cost, revenue, and profit:

Example:

A company has fixed costs of $1000 and variable costs of $5 per unit. The cost function is:

\( C(x) = 5x + 1000 \)

If the selling price is $15 per unit, the revenue function is:

\( R(x) = 15x \)

The profit function is:

\( P(x) = R(x) - C(x) = 15x - (5x + 1000) = 10x - 1000 \)

Quadratic Functions

A quadratic function has the form:

\( f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \)

where \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) are constants and \( a \neq 0 \). The graph of a quadratic function is a parabola.

Properties of Parabolas

Vertex
The highest or lowest point
\( x = -\frac{b}{2a} \)
Axis of Symmetry
Vertical line through the vertex
\( x = -\frac{b}{2a} \)
Direction
Opens up if \( a > 0 \), down if \( a < 0 \)
Y-intercept
Point where \( x = 0 \)
\( (0, c) \)

Business Application

Quadratic functions are used to model revenue when price affects demand:

Example:

A company finds that when it charges \( p \) dollars for a product, it sells \( 1000 - 50p \) units. The revenue function is:

\( R(p) = p(1000 - 50p) = 1000p - 50p^2 \)

This is a quadratic function. To find the price that maximizes revenue:

\( p = -\frac{b}{2a} = -\frac{1000}{2(-50)} = 10 \)

The maximum revenue is \( R(10) = 1000(10) - 50(10)^2 = 5000 \)

Study Tips for Success

For Economics

  • Relate concepts to real-world examples and current events
  • Practice drawing and interpreting supply and demand graphs
  • Focus on understanding the intuition behind economic models
  • Review key terms and their definitions regularly

For Mathematics

  • Practice solving problems regularly, not just before exams
  • Work through examples step by step before trying exercises
  • Focus on understanding concepts, not just memorizing formulas
  • Use graphing to visualize functions and relationships
